Borovia

The mountain kingdom of Borovia is home to the Borovian Dwarves. Ambitious but ambiguous in their intentions, the Borovian Dwarves are an insular culture who do not always take kindly to outlanders - unless they have gold, goods, or minerals.

The capital of Borovia is the City of Borovia. Hewn from the face of the forbidding Obsidian Mountain, the city features terraced walls topped with wide parapets. The city center features sturdy buildings made from enormous and expertly-quarried rock; the edges of the city are carved into the mountain itself. Traders and outsiders are allowed into the city, but none are allowed to pass the Gilded Gate, which leads deep into the earth.

History

The Kingdom of Borovia was founded eons ago by the earliest Borovian Dwarves. In the aftermath of The Calamity, the Borovian Dwarves constructed elaborate fortifications in the mountains and began tunneling into the earth to extract minerals and build an impenetrable underground fortress. Realizing that there was little to no competition for space or resources below ground, the Dwarves improved their surface-level defenses to keep outsiders at bay and focused their energy on tunneling ever further into the earth.

Politics and Social Structure

The Borovian Dwarves are led by a dynastic monarchy, the Deepdiggers. The City of Borovia is the diplomatic hub of the kingdom: it is the singular point of commerce and trade with the Borovian Dwarves, and it is the singular point of contact for diplomatic relationships.


The current king of the Borovian Dwarves is Niall Deepdigger. He has maintained the insular culture of the Dwarves, but maintained diplomatic relationships with the humans in the [[Kingdom of Breland]] and the Elves of the [[Western Isles]].


Borovian Society is a mix of a dynastic monarchy and a merit-based society. Living in mines, tunnels, and chambers, and working with ores and minerals, rewards the most skilled and hardest-working among the Dwarves. After all, poorly engineered tunnels collapse and poorly alloyed metals fracture. The Dwarves are therefore very focused on results and pragmatism and are not generally susceptible to bullshit and politics.

Social Structure and Castes

The social structure of the Dwarves mirrors the physical structure of their Kingdom: a series of interconnected and interdependent nodes. The highest caste in Borovian society is the Royal Caste. Essentially the administrative class of the Kingdom, the Royal Caste is the diplomatic face of the Borovian Dwarves, and they organize the efforts of the Dwarves through a system of Chambers. Each Chamber has a Chamber Master, usually a member of the Royal Caste; the Chamber Masters orchestrate trade and coordinate sharing of resources and maintenance with adjacent Chambers.

The Royal Guard is a subcaste of the Royals. These are warriors, trained to fight in Chambers and in surface warfare. In surface and subterranean warfare, they rely on stealth, cunning, and brutality. They are known to use nets, pitfalls, and high-pressure water-and-air cannons. The Royal Guard acts as an internal police force for the Dwarves, and patrols are assigned to each Chamber.

Below the Royal Caste is the Designer Caste: the Dwarven engineers. Dig Designers design Chambers, Tunnels, and Mines; Tool Designers design tools, weapons, and machines; and Social Designers organize the biological needs (ventilation, food, drink, shelter, exchange, recreation) of the Dwarves in each Chamber.

Below the Designer Caste is the Builder Caste. These are the Dwarves who build: the artisans, craftsmen, smiths, diggers, farmers, bakers, plumbers and pipefitters, and other skilled workers who make day-to-day life in the Chambers function.

Sitting apart from the other castes is the Provider Caste. These dwarves venture into The Deeps or onto the Surface on hunting and fishing excursions.

Crime and Justice

Severe crimes, such as sabotaging Chamber infrastructure or Treason, generally lead to spectacular executions by magma pit: the convicted is marched out to the edge of the pit and physically hurled into the magma by Royal Guards. Since Dwarven society is unusually reliant on mutual trust and competency, Dwarves convicted of Fraud or Theft are usually left suspended in iron cages over the Magma Pits, where onlookers or victims can taunt them as they are slowly and painfully roasted alive. Higher crimes such murder, and problematic behavior such as habitual drunkenness or brawling, leads to one of two options: exile to the Surface, or exile to the Deeps, a seemingly endless series of unexplored underground caverns and tunnels. Exiles are ejected from the Dwarven kingdom with a single pack of resources and whatever personal goods they can carry. If an Exile wishes to return to Borovian society, they must prove themselves worthy by discovering, building, or obtaining something that is of material use to the Borovian Dwarves. If they are allowed to return, Exiles must spend a period of five years in Penance, where they are closely monitored. If they have harmed a specific individual or clan, those five years are spent in servitude to those persons.

Chambers

The Dwarves have installed a special electromagnet in Borovia, and use cunning compasses oriented to Borovian North, combined with depth gauges, to describe the locations of their Chambers. Chambers therefore have names like 9k-west-65m-deep: this Chamber would be 9 kilometers west of Borovia and 65 meters deeper.

Chambers are designed to be highly redundant and self-sufficient in the event of collapse, earthquake, or other catastrophe. While every Chamber has independent ventilation shafts, only some Chambers have a direct egress point to the surface. The locations of ingress/egress points are carefully guarded by the Dwarves. Every Chamber has:

  • One Great Hall,
  • One Chamber Master who oversees operations,
  • One detachment of Royal Guards,
  • Three redundant ventilation shafts,
  • Three redundant water sources,
  • Three farms.

Great Halls are the centers of Dwarven social life. They are where Dwarves gather to eat, drink, celebrate, and socialize. The Chamber Master and Royal Guards occupy these halls.

Dwarven residences are carved from the rock. Usually residences hold most members of a specific Clan, so they are generally large and spacious, with multiple sub-chambers. Dwarven marriage rituals involve contests of skill: the couple's Clans hold contests, judged by the Chamber Master of an unrelated Chamber, to determine which Clan will retain the married couple.

Dwarven farmers cultivate meticulously bred fungi and crops that thrive in low-light or no-light environments. They also maintain livestock - rabbits, fowl, and goats - that have been systematically bred since the Dwarves went belowground centuries ago. Some Chambers have Breeding Ponds where they grow fish. Provider dwarves occasionally bring wild game from the surface, but this is usually reserved for special occasions.

Each Chamber is connected to other Chambers by a series of tunnels. Maintenance in the Tunnels is shared by the connected Chambers. Uncooperative Chamber Masters, or those who shirk their duties, can be unseated by a consortium of their fellows.

Allies

The Borovian Dwarves do not have any formal surface-level alliances. However, they do maintain trade and diplomatic relationships with the Elves of the Western Isles and the Kingdom of Breland. They are friendly with the Halflings of Bramblewood; they seem to see the Halflings as a curiosity, as they reside both in burrows and in trees.

Enemies

Surface Enemies

Orcs, Goblins, Kobolds. Cave- and mountain-dwelling creatures such as Kobolds, Orcs, and Goblins are often antagonistic to the Dwarves. They will collapse or sabotage ventilation shafts and will sometimes raid Chambers.

Bandits. Bandits and tribesmen in the wilds will occasionally mount attacks or raids on Dwarven ingress/egress points, which is why the Dwarves are so secretive about these locations.

Deep Enemies

Myconids. Some Myconid colonies are neutral or even friendly to the Dwarves, but some are aggressive or antagonistic. Spore attacks or raids are uncommon, but hostile Myconids will periodically abduct Dwarves from deep within mines or shafts.

Insects. Deep-dwelling centipedes, spiders, and ants cause havoc in Dwarven mines and Chambers.

Elementals. Earth Elementals are generally neutral, but will occasionally guard or defend specific seams of ore or minerals.
